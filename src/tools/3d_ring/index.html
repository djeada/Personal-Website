üìö Mathematics of the Torus

üîò What is a Torus?

A torus (plural: tori) is a surface of revolution generated by revolving a circle in three-dimensional space about an axis that is coplanar with the circle. If the axis does not touch the circle, the surface has a ring shape and is called a ring torus.

The torus is one of the most important surfaces in mathematics and has applications in topology, geometry, and physics. In everyday life, it's the shape of a donut, bagel, or ring!

üåê Torus Types

üî∏ Ring Torus (R > r)

The standard doughnut shape where the tube doesn't intersect itself. This is what we visualize in this tool. The "hole" in the center has a radius of R - r.

üîπ Horn Torus (R = r)

The limiting case where the tube just touches itself at the center. The hole shrinks to a single point.

üîª Spindle Torus (R < r)

When the minor radius exceeds the major radius, the surface intersects itself, creating a self-intersecting spindle shape.

üí° 3D Rendering Concepts

This visualization uses several fundamental 3D graphics techniques:

  • Perspective Projection: Creates the illusion of depth by making distant points appear smaller
  • Phong Shading: Calculates lighting at each point using ambient, diffuse, and specular components
  • Normal Vectors: Perpendicular vectors to the surface used for lighting calculations
  • Z-Buffering: Ensures correct rendering order so near surfaces hide far ones
  • Rotation Matrices: Transform 3D coordinates to achieve interactive rotation

üéØ Applications

Tori appear throughout mathematics, science, and engineering:

  • Topology: The torus is a fundamental object in algebraic topology, having genus 1 (one hole)
  • Physics: Magnetic confinement in tokamak fusion reactors uses toroidal geometry
  • Computer Graphics: Tori are commonly used to test rendering algorithms
  • Architecture: Toroidal structures appear in bridges, buildings, and stadiums
  • Video Games: Some game worlds are topologically equivalent to a torus (wrapping in both directions)
